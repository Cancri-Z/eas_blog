WHAT'S YOUR CAR TIRE TREAD IS TELLING YOU?



Tire tread refers to the pattern of grooves and channels on the surface of a tire that comes into contact with the road. The design of the tread pattern plays a crucial role in the tire's performance, including traction, handling, braking, and wear resistance. Your tires are your car's only connection to the road. They're overlooked as safety items, but sometimes they're the first place where a problem will appear. We show you how to read your tires and know what the warning signs mean. Tires are the silent, nameless heroes of your car, and your personal safety. They’re often overlooked as vital features to a vehicle, particularly as safety items, thanks to all the other neat accessories cars have these days (i.e. cup holders).

As your car’s only connection to the road, your tires can tell you more than the conditions of the road. They’re often the first place a potential car problem can appear, making it important that you know how to ‘read’ your tires. We’ll offer insight on what your tires can be telling you, and what these warning signs could mean. Tires can be windows to the health of your car—if you know how to look. Some may find this hard to believe. But, tires are sensitive. They begin to wear differently if something isn’t right, which means your tire tread is trying to tell you something. This can include inflation issues, issues with your driving, alignment problems and even problems in your suspension or brake systems.

Your tires can handle a lot, ranging from roads covered in mild or heavy downpour of rain to scorching sun pavement, but there is a limit to how long they can safely get you from point A to point B. The average tire is designed to drive around 50,000 miles, but that number can vary based on different factors. As the only parts of the car that physically touch the ground, tires are one of the key factors affecting a vehicle’s overall highway safety. Being able to recognize the signs of worn tires can help keep you and your passengers safe, no matter where the road takes you.



TYPES OF CAR TIRE TREADS WEAR AND HOW TO FIX IT



1. Center Tire Tread Wear: Take a peek at your tires. If you see the center strip of your tire is more worn than the sides or edges of the tire that means your tire is overinflated. Overinflation causes tires to bulge in the center, meaning this is the spot on the tire connecting the most with the road.

The Repair: Your tire comes with inflation guidelines. It is important to follow your tires’ and car’s recommended pounds per square inch(PSI). The recommended tire psi can be found on the outer wall of your tire, on a sticker found inside the front passenger door or in your owner’s manual.



2. Side/Shoulder Tire Tread Wear: If your tires are showing wear on the sides/edges/shoulders more than the center, this is a result of underinflation. Your tires are not as spherical as they should be causing the sides to take most of the weight.

The Repair: Once again, follow your tires’ and car’s recommended pounds per square inch (PSI).



3. Wear on One Side: If your tires are wearing unevenly on one side or another, and your tires are properly inflated, it may mean your tires are out of alignment, causing your cars to ride on one side more heavily.

The Repair: Wearing on one side can be addressed by taking your car in for a wheel realignment.



4. Feathering: Feathering is hard to notice, but it can be felt when running your hands along your tire. Feathering describes when individual tread blocks begin to resemble a ramp, occurring in a sideways direction. One side of the block will be flat, gradually getting thicker until the steep, angled drop-off on the other side. Feathering is a result of excessive toeing of your tires. The toeing is causing your tires to interact to the road like cheese interacts with a grader. Feathering can also be a result of aggressive driving and driving around corners too quickly.

The Repair: The problem can be fixed with a wheel realignment.



5. Heel-Toe Wear: Like feathering, but the ramp runs from front to back, not side to side. Heel-Toe wear is most likely a result of infrequent or neglecting to rotate your tires regularly.

The Repair: Have your tires rotated more frequently. Many mechanics and car technicians will automatically rotate tires during regular oil changes.



6. Tire Cupping/Scalloping Edges: Tire cupping (or scalloping) is apparent when your tire’s outer rim has what look like alternating hills and valleys. According to Popular Mechanics, tire cupping happens when your suspension is bent, or your shock absorbers aren’t working correctly you may feel your car is bouncing as you travel down the road.

The Repair: Have a mechanic check your shock absorbers and suspension.



7. Outer-Edge Wear: If your tires are wearing on the outer edge, your tires may be consistently underinflated. Underinflating your tires causes more weight from the car to flatten the tire and shift more weight to the outer treads.

The Repair: Rotate tires regularly, and make sure tires are inflated to the level recommended by the tire manufacturer.



8. Inner-Edge Wear: When the inner side/edge is showing more wear this is an indication of negative camber or toe-in alignment issues.

The Repair: Check for exccessive negative camber or toe-in.



9. Flat Spots Wear: If your tires are showing normal wear, but have a noticeable smooth spot or spots, this is flat spot wear. It is a result of emergency braking. However, it can also indicate brake problems and issue with your brake’s foundation. If you do not recall taking a sudden stop recently, have your vehicle’s brakes checked immediately.

The Repair: Check your brakes.



10. Sidewall While not directly involved with tire tread, if you notice sidewall damage to your tire the solution is simple.

The Repair: Stop parking so close to curbs.


TREAD DEPTH

Tread depth is the vertical measurement from the top of the tread rubber to the bottom of the tire's deepest grooves. It's typically measured in millimeters (mm) or inches (in). Adequate tread depth is essential for maintaining traction on wet or slippery roads. In many places, there are legal requirements for minimum tread depth to ensure safe driving conditions.


TREAD PATTERNS

Tread patterns vary widely depending on the intended use of the tire. Common tread patterns include:

1. Symmetric Tread: These treads have the same pattern on both sides and are often found on standard, all-season tires.

2. Asymmetric Tread: These treads have different patterns on the inner and outer sides of the tire, designed to optimize performance in various road conditions, such as wet and dry roads.

3. Directional Tread: These treads have a specific direction of rotation and are optimized for water evacuation to prevent hydroplaning. They typically feature V-shaped or arrow-shaped grooves.

4. Winter Tread: Winter tires have unique tread patterns with deep grooves and sipes (small cuts) designed to provide better traction on snow and ice.

GROOVES AND SIPES

Grooves are the large channels carved into the tread, while sipes are the smaller cuts or slots. These features help to evacuate water from under the tire, reducing the risk of hydroplaning and improving grip on wet surfaces. Sipes also provide additional biting edges for traction in snow and ice.



TREAD WEAR INDICATORS

Many tires have built-in tread wear indicators, also known as wear bars or wear indicators, located in the grooves. These indicators become visible as the tread wears down, serving as a visual cue that it's time to replace the tire.

TREAD LIFE

The lifespan of a tire's tread depends on various factors, including driving habits, road conditions, tire maintenance, and tire quality. Regularly inspecting the tread depth and condition of your tires is essential for safe driving and optimal performance.

ROTATING TIRES

Rotating tires at regular intervals helps to ensure even wear across all tires, extending their lifespan and maintaining consistent performance.


CAR TIRE TREAD MAINTENANCE

Maintaining proper tire tread is crucial for ensuring safe driving conditions and optimal performance of your vehicle. Proper tire maintenance, including regular inflation checks, alignment, and balancing, can help maximize tread life and ensure safe driving conditions. Here's a list of general maintenance tasks related to car tire tread:

1. Regular Inspections: Conduct visual inspections of your tires regularly, ideally once a month or before long trips. Look for signs of uneven wear, damage, or foreign objects embedded in the tread.

2. Check Tread Depth: Measure the tread depth using a tread depth gauge or the built-in tread wear indicators (wear bars). Ensure that the tread depth meets the legal requirements and is sufficient for safe driving. Replace tires if the tread depth is worn down to the wear bars or if it's below the recommended minimum depth.

3. Monitor Tire Pressure: Maintaining proper tire pressure is essential for even wear and optimal performance. Check tire pressure at least once a month and adjust it according to the manufacturer's recommendations or the specifications listed on the vehicle's tire placard (usually located on the driver's door jamb or in the owner's manual).

4. Rotate Tires: Regularly rotate your tires according to the manufacturer's recommendations, typically every 5,000 to 7,000 miles (or as specified in your vehicle's owner's manual). Rotating tires promotes even wear and extends their lifespan.

5. Alignment and Balancing: Ensure that your vehicle's wheels are properly aligned and balanced. Misalignment or unbalanced tires can cause uneven tread wear, leading to premature tire replacement and affecting vehicle handling.

6. Avoid Overloading: Avoid overloading your vehicle beyond its recommended weight capacity. Excessive weight can put additional strain on tires, leading to accelerated tread wear.

7. Drive Carefully: Practice safe driving habits to minimize unnecessary wear on your tires. Avoid harsh braking, acceleration, and cornering, which can cause premature tire wear.

8. Avoid Rough Surfaces: Try to avoid driving over rough or uneven surfaces whenever possible, as they can cause damage to the tire tread.

9. Inspect for Damage: Regularly inspect your tires for signs of damage, such as cuts, bulges, or punctures. If you notice any damage, have the tire inspected by a professional and replaced if necessary.

10. Consider Seasonal Tires: In regions with significant seasonal weather changes, consider using seasonal tires (e.g., winter tires) to ensure optimal traction and safety in different driving conditions.
